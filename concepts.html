Key Concepts in Sartrean Existentialism

Existence Precedes Essence

This foundational principle asserts that human beings are not born with a predetermined essence or nature. Unlike objects designed for a specific purpose, humans must create their own identity through conscious action.

Freedom

Sartre emphasizes radical human freedom. Individuals are always free to choose, even under conditions of extreme constraint. This freedom is inseparable from responsibility, as every choice reflects a personal commitment to a particular vision of humanity.

Responsibility

For Sartre, responsibility extends beyond the individual. By choosing oneself, one simultaneously chooses for all humanity. This universalizing dimension gives existentialist ethics its seriousness and weight.

Anguish

Anguish arises from the awareness of one’s radical freedom and responsibility. It is not a pathological state but a philosophical recognition of the burden of choice.

Bad Faith

Bad faith refers to the denial of one’s own freedom, often by adopting fixed social roles or external authorities as excuses. Sartre critiques bad faith as a form of self-deception that undermines authentic existence.
