Key Concepts

Core Ideas in Sartrean Existentialism

Existence Precedes Essence

Sartre’s most famous concept asserts that human beings exist before they acquire any defined essence or nature. Individuals are not born with predetermined purposes but must create meaning through action.

Freedom

Human freedom is central to Sartre’s philosophy. Even in situations of extreme constraint, individuals remain free in their capacity to choose how they respond.

Responsibility

With freedom comes responsibility. Sartre argues that individuals are responsible not only for themselves but also for humanity as a whole, since each action affirms a vision of what human beings ought to be.

Anguish

Anguish arises from the recognition of one’s absolute responsibility. It is not a psychological weakness but a rational response to freedom.

Bad Faith

Bad faith refers to the denial of one’s own freedom, often through self-deception. Individuals may hide behind social roles or external authorities to avoid moral responsibility.
