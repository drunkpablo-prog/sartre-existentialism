Europe After World War II

Existentialism Is a Humanism was delivered in 1945, immediately after the end of World War II. Europe was emerging from a period of unprecedented destruction, moral collapse, and political uncertainty. Traditional sources of meaning—religion, nationalism, and Enlightenment rationalism—had been deeply shaken.

In this historical moment, many intellectuals questioned whether universal moral systems or metaphysical foundations could still be sustained. The experience of war, occupation, and collaboration forced individuals to confront questions of guilt, responsibility, and choice in concrete, often tragic circumstances.

The Intellectual Climate of Postwar France

In France, existentialism emerged as both a philosophical movement and a cultural phenomenon. Writers and philosophers such as Jean-Paul Sartre, Simone de Beauvoir, and Albert Camus addressed a broad public audience rather than an exclusively academic one.

Existentialism emphasized lived experience, individual freedom, and moral responsibility. It rejected abstract systems detached from human life and instead focused on concrete human situations.

Existentialism as a Public Philosophy

Sartre’s lecture was explicitly addressed to critics who accused existentialism of promoting despair or moral relativism. By presenting existentialism as a form of humanism, Sartre sought to defend the ethical seriousness of his philosophy.

The lecture thus reflects a broader postwar effort to reconstruct ethical meaning without reliance on transcendental guarantees. Human beings, according to Sartre, must assume full responsibility for the values they create.
