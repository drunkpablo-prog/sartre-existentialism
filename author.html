Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980) was a French philosopher, novelist, playwright, and public intellectual. He is widely regarded as one of the central figures of twentieth-century existentialism.

Sartre’s philosophy is deeply shaped by phenomenology, particularly the work of Edmund Husserl and Martin Heidegger. His major philosophical works include Being and Nothingness (1943) and Existentialism Is a Humanism (1945).

Beyond philosophy, Sartre was actively engaged in political and social debates. He famously refused the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1964, arguing that a writer should not become an institution.
