Existentialism Is a Humanism

Origin of the Text

Existentialism Is a Humanism originated as a public lecture delivered by Jean-Paul Sartre in Paris in 1945. The lecture was intended as a response to widespread misunderstandings and criticisms of existentialist philosophy, particularly accusations that existentialism promotes despair, moral nihilism, or political irresponsibility.

Purpose and Audience

Sartre’s primary aim was to clarify existentialism for a general audience. Rather than presenting a technical philosophical treatise, the lecture adopts a polemical and accessible tone. Sartre defends existentialism as a philosophy that emphasizes human freedom, responsibility, and ethical seriousness.

Main Argument

The central thesis of the work is that “existence precedes essence.” Sartre argues that human beings are not born with a fixed nature or predetermined purpose. Instead, individuals first exist and subsequently define themselves through their actions.

From this premise, Sartre derives a moral framework in which individuals are fully responsible not only for themselves but also for humanity as a whole. Every choice, he argues, implicitly affirms a vision of what human beings ought to be.

Reception and Influence

Upon publication, Existentialism Is a Humanism became one of Sartre’s most widely read works. While some philosophers criticized its simplifications, the text played a crucial role in popularizing existentialist thought and shaping post-war intellectual culture in Europe and beyond.
