Existentialism Is a Humanism (1945)

Origin of the Lecture

Existentialism Is a Humanism originated as a public lecture delivered by Jean-Paul Sartre in Paris in October 1945. The lecture was later published in written form and became one of Sartre’s most widely read philosophical texts.

Unlike Sartre’s more technical philosophical works, this lecture was intended for a general audience. Its aim was to clarify existentialism and to respond to public misunderstandings surrounding the movement.

Central Philosophical Claim

The central thesis of the work is the claim that “existence precedes essence.” Sartre argues that human beings are not born with a predefined nature or purpose. Instead, individuals must define themselves through their actions and choices.

This claim directly challenges philosophical and theological traditions that assume a fixed human essence determined by God, nature, or reason.

Ethical Implications

Sartre emphasizes that radical human freedom entails radical responsibility. By choosing oneself, one also chooses an image of humanity as a whole. Moral responsibility is therefore inescapable.

In this sense, the work presents existentialism not as a philosophy of despair, but as a serious ethical position grounded in human freedom.
